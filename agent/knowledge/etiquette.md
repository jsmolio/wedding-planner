# Wedding Etiquette Guide

## Invitations
- Invitations should be sent 6-8 weeks before the wedding date.
- Include a clear RSVP deadline, typically 3-4 weeks before the event.
- Address envelopes formally: "Mr. and Mrs. John Smith" or use both full
  names for unmarried couples.
- If children are not invited, address the envelope to the parents only and
  note "adults only" on the RSVP card.

## Plus-Ones
- Engaged or married partners should always receive a plus-one.
- Long-term partners (living together or dating 6+ months) typically receive
  a plus-one.
- Single guests *may* receive a plus-one at the couple's discretion, but it is
  not required.

## Seating Arrangements
- The head table traditionally seats the couple, the wedding party, and their
  partners.
- Seat guests who know each other at the same table to encourage conversation.
- Keep feuding family members at separate tables — ideally on opposite sides of
  the room.
- Ensure elderly or mobility-impaired guests are seated near exits and away from
  speakers.

## Gift Etiquette
- Register for gifts at a range of price points.
- Thank-you notes should be sent within three months of receiving a gift — and
  within two weeks of the honeymoon for gifts received at the wedding.
- Mention the specific gift in each thank-you note.

## Dietary Considerations
- Always ask about dietary restrictions on the RSVP card.
- Provide at least one vegetarian option regardless of guest responses.
- Label dishes clearly at the reception so guests with allergies can self-serve
  safely.
- Inform the caterer of severe allergies (nut, shellfish, gluten) well in
  advance.

## Speeches and Toasts
- The traditional order is: best man, maid of honour, parents of the couple,
  then the couple themselves.
- Keep speeches to 3-5 minutes.
- Avoid embarrassing stories or inside jokes the audience won't understand.
- Thank the hosts and acknowledge the wedding party.

## Day-Of Etiquette
- The ceremony should start on time — aim for no more than 5 minutes late.
- Phones should be silenced; consider an "unplugged ceremony" sign.
- Guests should not wear white or anything that could overshadow the couple.
- Tipping vendors at the end of the night is customary and appreciated.
